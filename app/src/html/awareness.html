Organ Donation & Legal Framework

Organ donation means giving part of body (organ) to a person with end stage organ disease who needs a transplant. The organs that can be donated include kidney, liver, heart, lungs, and small bowel and tissues such as corneas, heart valves, skin and bone.

Legal Framework In India

Organ donation in India is regulated by the Transplantation of Human Organs and Tissues Act, 1994. The National Organ and Tissue Transplant Organisation (NOTTO) functions as the apex body. India performs the second largest number of transplants in the world but still lags in donation rates (0.65 pmp) due to its huge population and lack of public awareness.

Types of Donors

Living Donors

Living donors are permitted to donate one kidney, a portion of the pancreas, or part of the liver. They must be over 18 years of age and usually donate to immediate blood relatives.

  • Near Related: Parents, siblings, children, spouse, etc.
  • Non-near relative: Donors other than near relatives, usually for special affection.
  • SWAP Donors: Pairing of donor-recipient sets to match compatibility.

Deceased Donors

Deceased donor is anyone who becomes a donor after Death (Brainstem/Cardiac). Deceased donors may donate six life-saving organs: kidneys, liver, heart, lungs, pancreas, and intestine. One brainstem death donor can save up to 37 different organs and tissues.

How Organ Donation Works

The process of organ donation consists of 6 major phases to ensure the lifesaving gift reaches those in need safely and ethically.

Phase 1: Sign-Up

Organ transplants cannot occur without those who generously sign up to be organ donors through state registries or driver's license renewals.

Phase 2: Critical Care

Hospital staff prioritize saving the patient's life. Only after all lifesaving efforts are exhausted and death is confirmed is donation considered.

Phase 3: Identification & Consent

Local organ procurement organizations (OPO) confirm potential for donation and seek consent from next of kin if not already registered.

Phase 4: Organ Transport

Skilled surgeons recover organs, which are then rushed to the recipient's hospital, often using "Green Corridors" for speed.

Phase 5: Organ Transplant

Transplantation surgery begins immediately upon arrival, as the recipient is typically already waiting in the operating room.

Phase 6: Recovery

Post-surgery recovery involves monitoring for rejection and infection, with medical teams supporting the recipient's return to health.
